The core failure

A person gets screened at an ASHA camp, is told their BP is elevated — and the handoff to enrolment in the NCD clinic at the PHC simply never happens. No one follows up. No one knows. Referral completion rates are estimated because they are almost never systematically measured.

Act 3 · Design Principles

The DPI Design Philosophy

CCE follows the same design principles that made Aadhaar, UPI, Beckn, DEPA, and Estonia's X-Road work at population scale:

①
Thin centre, thick edges
The central layer coordinates — it doesn't accumulate data or make decisions. Edge systems retain full autonomy.
②
Open protocol specifications
Anyone who speaks the protocol can participate. No vendor lock-in, no single platform dependency.
③
Federated trust
Trust via cryptographic attestation — not by a central authority holding all information.
④
Data stays at the edges
The centre sees only coordination metadata — never clinical records, never patient data.
⑤
Progressive enhancement
Works at the lowest common denominator. Gets richer as participants mature — no big-bang required.
1.5 minutes. With Pramod: don't over-explain these principles — he designed most of them himself. Instead, frame it as: "We've taken the same constraints that made your DPI work and asked: what does this look like for health coordination?" The key insight is that CCE is applying a proven pattern to a new domain — not inventing new principles.

Act 4 · Architecture

Two Reporting Mechanisms

Trust Mechanism
1. Counter-Party Deviation Reporting
  • Both sides of a care transition independently attest via signed hashes
  • Normal case is silent — agreement is the default, nothing flows centrally
  • Only when a counter-party flags a deviation does a signal reach the central layer
  • Loss-to-follow-up detection is free — a referral with no counter-party confirmation is a patient who fell through
The absence of a deviation signal is itself proof that coordination is working.
Observation, Intelligence & Policy
2. Policy-Based Event Reporting & Coordination Alerts
  • Edge adapter consults a reporting policy (pushed from governance) determining what metadata flows centrally
  • A spectrum, not binary:
    → Aggregates only (weekly referral counts)
    → Individual events for notifiable diseases + aggregates for routine care
    → Full instrumentation for pilot sites
  • Low-maturity regions start at aggregate end; progress as capacity grows
  • Generates coordination-layer alerts back to the edge — unacknowledged referral flags, SLA breach notifications, follow-up due signals — without touching clinical decisions
The centre never sees clinical content — only event types, provider IDs, timestamps, hashed plan references.
2 minutes. This slide is architecturally important — distinguish the two mechanisms clearly. Mechanism 1 is about trust: it's exception-driven and inherently lightweight. Mechanism 2 is about observation: it's configurable and policy-governed. The key design insight is that these are separate concerns, handled separately. Pramod may ask: "How does L3 compute population dashboards if it only sees deviations?" — that's exactly why they're separate: deviations are for trust, event reporting is for observation.

CCE Protocol Design: Verbs & Nouns

Coordination Verbs  (generalised — applicable beyond health)
transition
Initiate a handoff of responsibility between parties
accept / decline
Receiving party responds to a transition
attest
Cryptographically sign that a coordination event occurred
flag-deviation
Counter-party reports a discrepancy
sync-plan
Share/update a shared plan across party boundaries
report
Emit a metadata event per reporting policy
update-policy
Governance pushes new configuration to edges
Health Coordination Nouns  (domain-specific resources)
CareTransition
Referral or handoff; composes FHIR ServiceRequest
CarePlan
Shared versioned plan; composes FHIR CarePlan (hashed ref only flows centrally)
CareAttestation
Signed hash of care event; composes FHIR Encounter hash + credentials
CareDeviationReport
Flagged discrepancy between counter-parties
ReportingPolicy
Configuration governing what metadata edges emit
CareCoordinationEvent
Observation signal; constrained FHIR profile, clinical content stripped
Relationship to UHI / Beckn: CCE leverages UHI (built on Beckn) as the discovery and appointment layer — finding providers, booking referral slots, confirming availability. CCE adds what UHI doesn't cover: longitudinal coordination — care plan sync, counter-party attestation, deviation reporting, policy-governed observation. UHI handles "find and book"; CCE handles "coordinate, attest, and observe over time." They are complementary layers.

Appendix A

Generalised Protocol Model

The coordination verbs are domain-neutral. Health specificity lives in the nouns. The binding model shows how health nouns compose generic coordination primitives.

Generic Noun (Protocol Core) Health Binding Underlying Data Structure
TransitionCareTransitionFHIR ServiceRequest, referral metadata
PlanCarePlanFHIR CarePlan, care team references
AttestationCareAttestationFHIR Encounter summary hash, provider credentials
DeviationCareDeviationReportReferral mismatch, follow-up failure metadata
MetadataEventCareCoordinationEventConstrained FHIR profiles (clinical content stripped)
Why this matters: The protocol core (verbs + generic nouns + trust model + reporting policy mechanism) is reusable. The same grammar could serve coordination in education (student transitions), social services (case handoffs), or supply chain (logistics handoffs). Health is the first domain binding — not a near-term commitment, but a deliberate architectural affordance.

Analogues: HTTP verbs are domain-neutral, REST resources are domain-specific. Beckn's coordination grammar is domain-neutral, catalogue schema is vertical-specific. CCE follows the same pattern.
